Discrete Fourier Transform

Think of the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) like testing your signal against a set of tuning forks. You mathematically interact with the signal using different "probe" frequencies to measure which ones resonate and how strongly.

Part 1: The Core Intuition (Resonance via Correlation)

How does the math actually test a tuning fork? It uses correlation (which mathematically is a form of inner product). We multiply our unknown Target Signal by a known Sine Probe Signal, point by point, and calculate the integral (average) of the result over a time window.

  • If the frequencies do not match, the positive and negative parts continuously cancel out, and the result's average is zero.
  • If the frequencies match perfectly, the waves "resonate." The result becomes a constant (a DC component) whose value is proportional to the signal's amplitude.

The Deep Reason: Why does the average perfectly cancel out?

Instead of thinking about multiplying two wavy lines, we can view this geometrically using Euler's formula:

ejωt = cos(ωt) + j·sin(ωt)

This equation represents a unit vector rotating in the complex plane. When you multiply a target signal (ejω₁t) by a probe signal (e-jω₂t), the product becomes a single rotating vector spinning at the difference of their frequencies: Δω = ω₁ - ω₂.

Case 1: Different Frequencies (Δω ≠ 0)

The vector is spinning. Averaging the signal means integrating (adding up) all the directions of this vector over time:

  • Right cancels left
  • Up cancels down

Think of walking 1 step East, 1 North, 1 West, 1 South: (1,0) + (0,1) + (-1,0) + (0,-1) = 0.

Assumption: Perfect cancellation ONLY happens if the signal completes an exact, integer number of cycles within our observation window. If we cut it off halfway through a circle, it won't equal exactly zero!

Case 2: Matching Frequencies (Δω = 0)

Because Δω = 0, the math becomes ej0 = 1. The vector stops rotating and permanently points in one direction.

Now you are just adding: 1 + 1 + 1 + ... The vector's length accumulates steadily over time. This is how the math logically locks onto a matching frequency without interference.

Part 2: The Inner Product

There is one catch. Try matching the frequencies in the Part 1 animation, but move the Phase Shift slider to exactly 90°. The average drops back to zero! A single probe is "blind" to a signal shifted by 90 degrees.

To solve this, Euler's complex math provides two cameras placed 90 degrees apart (a cosine sensor and a sine sensor). By capturing both "shadows" and using the Pythagorean theorem, the true amplitude is successfully rebuilt regardless of the starting phase.

Part 3: The Grand Synthesis

Everything we have done—multiplying by tuning forks, rotating vectors, and using two orthogonal cameras to capture shadows—builds to one unifying mathematical concept.

The Core Idea of DFT

  • Because a digital signal is made of N samples, it can be thought of as existing in an N-dimensional space.
  • Each basis frequency acts as a unique, orthogonal direction (axis) within that space.
  • The DFT measures exactly how much of your signal lies in each direction.

This mathematical projection is exactly what we did in Part 1: multiplying the signal by a probe and averaging it over time is computing an inner product. Written as one elegant summation, it looks like this:

Xk = Σ xn · e-j(2πkn/N)

Xk: The output complex number for frequency bin k. Its magnitude tells you how strong that frequency is, and its angle tells you the phase (where the wave starts).

xn: Your raw sampled data points over time.

e-j(...): The combined Sine/Cosine orthogonal rotating basis spinning at frequency k.
Why the negative sign? It forces the mathematical probe to spin backwards. This perfectly "unwinds" the target frequency, freezing the vector at e0 = 1 so its magnitude can accumulate!

Part 4: The Periodic Assumption & Spectral Leakage

Adjust the frequency slider below. If you set the target to exactly 5.0 Hz, it completes exactly 5 cycles. If you looped this window, the wave would connect perfectly smoothly. It maintains perfect orthogonality.

But if you set it to 5.5 Hz, the wave gets cut off mid-cycle! If you looped this window, there would be a sharp, jarring jump at the boundary. Because of this discontinuity, it loses its perfect orthogonality with the other basis frequencies. The inner product projections are no longer zero for the other bins, so the energy "leaks" out, creating a smeared "skirt" effect.
