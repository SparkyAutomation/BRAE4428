Fourier series

A Fourier series says that a repeating pattern can be rebuilt from simple sine and cosine waves. This demo shows the idea with animation and a live simulation using classic signals such as square, sawtooth, triangle, and smooth periodic waves.

Core idea

If a signal repeats every period T, Fourier series rewrites it as a stack of smooth waves. The low-frequency waves capture the broad shape, and the higher-frequency waves add sharp corners and fine detail.

f(t) ≈ a₀ + Σ [aₙ cos(nωt) + bₙ sin(nωt)],   ω = 2π/T
  • a₀: the average level.
  • Low harmonics: slow seasonal or daily swings.
  • High harmonics: sharper edges and finer detail in the waveform.

Use the animations below to see how adding harmonics changes the reconstructed signal.

How to interpret the terms

1. Average term: sets the vertical center of the signal.

2. First few harmonics: capture the broad overall shape.

3. Higher harmonics: add corners, ripples, and fine structure.

The Math of Phase Shifts: Combining Sine and Cosine

In the trigonometric formula above, every frequency n has two separate components: a cosine wave (aₙ) and a sine wave (bₙ). However, in the epicycle animation below, you don't see two separate circles for every frequency. You only see one circle per frequency. This is because a sine wave and a cosine wave of the same frequency can be mathematically combined into a single, shifted wave.

1. The Radius (Amplitude)

Think of aₙ and bₙ as the legs of a right triangle. The radius of the epicycle is the hypotenuse, found using the Pythagorean theorem:

R = √(aₙ² + bₙ²)

This explains why a harmonic with strong sine and cosine terms results in a large circle.

2. The Starting Angle (Phase Shift)

The ratio of aₙ to bₙ dictates the "head start" the circle needs to trace the correct wave. This angle, φ, is found using the inverse tangent:

φ = arctan(bₙ / aₙ)

If a harmonic is pure cosine, the circle starts at 0°. If it is pure sine, it starts at 90°. A mix of both drags the wave to align perfectly.

Euler Core Idea

The complex (Euler) form of the Fourier series treats a repeating signal as the horizontal motion of a chain of spinning vectors (arrows) connected tip-to-tail. By combining pairs of vectors that spin in opposite directions—one clockwise, one counter-clockwise—their vertical (imaginary) movements cancel each other out perfectly, leaving behind only the real, physical waveform.

f(t) = Σ cₙ einωt

How to interpret the complex terms

Σ (The Summation): Represents the "tip-to-tail" attachment of all circles. It counts from negative infinity to positive infinity.

c₀ (The Anchor): The stationary center point of the very first circle. It represents the overall average vertical level.

cₙ (The Blueprint): A single complex number that defines the radius (amplitude) and phase shift (starting angle at t=0) for the n-th circle.

einωt (The Engine): The mathematical motor that causes the circle to spin as time (t) moves forward.

n (The Speed & Direction): The frequency multiplier. Positive n spins counter-clockwise; negative n spins clockwise.
