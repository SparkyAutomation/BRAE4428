Why Square the Errors?

Raw errors cancel: +10 and −10 sum to zero, suggesting a "perfect" fit. Squaring makes all errors positive and heavily penalises outliers. The gradient (∂L/∂m) tells the algorithm exactly which direction to nudge m to shrink the loss fastest.

MSE vs R² — Two Lenses

MSE is in your data's units — useful for gradient updates. R² is unit-free (0–1): it tells you the fraction of variance your line explains. R²=0.85 → the model captures 85% of the variation. R²=0 → no better than predicting the mean every time.

Bias–Variance Trade-off

Try K=1 vs K=15. K=1 memorises every training point — the boundary is jagged and sensitive to noise (high variance). K=15 blurs everything — smooth but risks misclassifying complex regions (high bias). This tension exists in every ML model.

The Curse of Dimensionality

KNN works beautifully here in 2D. In 100+ dimensional space, all points become roughly equidistant — "nearest neighbour" loses meaning. This is why dimensionality reduction (PCA, t-SNE) is essential before applying KNN to real data.

Unsupervised · Clustering

K-Means Clustering — Finding Hidden Structure

K-Means gets data with no labels. It discovers structure through two alternating steps: Assign every point to the nearest centroid (★), then Move that centroid to the mean of its assigned members. Repeat until nothing changes.

Assignment — Voronoi Cells

The coloured background is a Voronoi diagram — every pixel belongs to its nearest centroid. The boundaries shift as centroids move. At convergence, the Voronoi regions define the final clusters: any new point landing in a region joins that cluster.

WCSS Monotonically Decreases

Watch the WCSS value drop each step — it never increases. K-Means is mathematically guaranteed to converge, because both the assignment and update steps reduce or maintain J. However, it may find a local minimum, not the global optimum.

The Base Rate Fallacy

Set the Base Rate P(A) to 5%, and True Positives P(B|A) to 90%. Even with highly accurate evidence, a tiny False Positive rate (e.g., 10%) can completely dominate the results. Because the "Not A" area is so massive, 10% of a huge number dwarfs 90% of a tiny number. Your posterior probability will be surprisingly low.

How ML uses this — Naïve Bayes

Algorithms like Naïve Bayes use this exact intersection logic, but layered. P(A | features) ∝ P(A) · P(f₁|A) · P(f₂|A). Each new piece of evidence "slices" the area further. The algorithm assumes features intersect independently ("naively"), allowing it to rapidly multiply these fractions together to classify data.
