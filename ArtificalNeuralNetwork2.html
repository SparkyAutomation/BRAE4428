💡 Cross-Entropy Loss — why it works

The Formula: L = −∑ y·log(ŷ)

Cross-entropy uses a dot product to measure the similarity between the true answers and a logarithmic penalty scale—which ruthlessly punishes confident mistakes—and then applies a minus sign to flip that negative alignment score into a positive error.

Why log? Exponential penalty for confidence

The log function penalises confident wrong predictions exponentially. Being 99% wrong is catastrophically worse than being 60% wrong — the gradient is huge, forcing a large weight update. Being 99% right barely moves the weights at all. MSE would give a much weaker signal for the confident-wrong case.

Numeric example — true label is Z

P(Z) = 0.01  →  L = −log(0.01) = 4.605  🔴 huge loss, big gradient
P(Z) = 0.50  →  L = −log(0.50) = 0.693  🟠 uncertain, moderate gradient
P(Z) = 0.99  →  L = −log(0.99) = 0.010  🟢 tiny loss, tiny gradient

The network is confident and wrong at P(Z)=0.01 — loss is 460× larger than when it’s confident and right. This asymmetry drives fast learning: big mistakes produce big gradients, small mistakes barely move the weights.

Compare to MSE: (0−0.01)²=0.0001 vs (0−0.99)²=0.98. Cross-entropy’s log scale is far more sensitive to confident errors.

Softmax output. The two output neurons compete: their raw values are normalised so they always sum to 1.0, giving a proper probability distribution over classes. exp(z) ensures all values are positive before normalising.
Backpropagation. Error flows backwards: output → hidden → input weights. Each weight is nudged proportionally to how much it contributed to the mistake (chain rule). The softmax+cross-entropy combination gives a clean gradient: δ² = ŷ − y.
Vanishing gradients. Sigmoid squashes gradients as they pass through layers. Switch to ReLU — it passes gradients unchanged for positive values, training much faster on deeper networks.
Overfitting. With only 8 training samples, the network can memorise them perfectly. Add noisy samples to see how the decision boundary generalises to unseen inputs.

Translation invariance. If the Z shifts one pixel right, the ANN sees a completely different input vector. The CNN’s filter detects the diagonal stroke wherever it appears.
MaxPooling. Takes the strongest activation in each 2×2 region. Makes the representation robust to small shifts and reduces feature map size by 4×.
Feature hierarchy. In deeper CNNs, early filters detect edges, middle filters detect shapes, deep filters detect objects. Even our tiny 2-filter network learns meaningful edge detectors.
Backprop through conv. Gradients flow back through pooling (only to the max position), through ReLU (zero where input was negative), and through convolution (cross-correlation with flipped filter).

When to use ANN

Use a fully-connected ANN when your data is tabular (rows and columns with no spatial relationship), when inputs are small and fixed-size, or when you need a simple baseline. Examples: predicting house prices from features, classifying customer churn, any structured dataset where pixel position doesn’t matter.

When to use CNN

Use a CNN whenever your data has local spatial structure — images, video frames, audio spectrograms, time-series signals. The convolutional layers learn to detect patterns (edges, textures, shapes) that are meaningful regardless of where they appear. CNNs are the backbone of all modern computer vision.

Key insight. On this tiny 5×5 dataset both networks can reach 100% accuracy. The CNN’s advantage becomes dramatic at scale: a 224×224 image has 50,176 pixels. An ANN with one hidden layer of 256 neurons needs 12.8M parameters. A CNN with the same depth needs only ~1M — and generalises far better to unseen images.
