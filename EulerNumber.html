Euler's Number

Discover how the constant of continuous growth bridges algebra, trigonometry, and complex geometry, leading to what many call the most beautiful equation in mathematics.

1. The "Goldilocks" Base (e)

All exponential functions of the form y = ax cross the y-axis at exactly 1. But notice how fast they are growing at that exact moment. Euler's number e (≈ 2.71828) is the exact numerical base required to make the slope perfectly equal to 1.

d/dx [ex] = ex

Because of this, it is the only function whose rate of growth is perfectly synchronized with its current value.

2. The Spectrum of ex

Depending on the exponent you provide, Euler's base controls entirely different transformations in nature and mathematics:

  • ex (x > 0): Continuous growth. Used to model population booms or compounding interest.
  • e-x (x < 0): Continuous decay. Used to model radioactive decay or a cooling cup of coffee.
  • e1: Just e itself (≈ 2.718). The growth factor after exactly one unit of time at 100% continuous growth.
  • eiθ (i is imaginary): Continuous rotation. Multiplying by i in the exponent pushes the growth sideways, tracing a circle in the complex plane.

Euler's Formula

When you feed an imaginary number into an exponential function, it perfectly links algebra with trigonometric geometry.

eiθ = cos(θ) + i·sin(θ)
  • Real part (cos): Horizontal position
  • Imaginary part (sin): Vertical position

3. Euler's Identity

If we pause the rotation at exactly half a circle (180 degrees, or π radians), the imaginary sine part becomes 0, and the real cosine part becomes -1. This gives us Euler's Identity, tying together five fundamental mathematical constants (0, 1, π, e, i) in one simple equation.

ei(0.00) = 1.00 + 0.00i
